Attribution is the psych/soc topic that shows up most reliably in one specific form: a passage describes someone explaining a behavior, and the question asks which bias is operating. Almost all of these resolve if you can track two things — whose behavior is being explained, and whether the explanation points inward or outward.

## The Basic Split

**Dispositional (internal) attribution** explains behavior by something about the person — personality, ability, effort, character. "He failed because he's lazy."

**Situational (external) attribution** explains behavior by circumstances — environment, luck, task difficulty, social pressure. "He failed because the exam was unfair."

That is the whole framework. Every bias below is a systematic tilt toward one of these two.

## Kelley's Covariation Model

The formal account of how people *should* assign attributions, using three dimensions:

**Consensus** — do other people behave this way in this situation? High consensus means everyone does it.

**Distinctiveness** — does this person behave this way only in this situation, or everywhere? High distinctiveness means it is specific to this situation.

**Consistency** — does this person behave this way in this situation repeatedly over time?

The combinations that matter:

- **High consensus + high distinctiveness + high consistency → situational.** Everyone does it, only here, every time. Something about the situation is causing it.
- **Low consensus + low distinctiveness + high consistency → dispositional.** Only this person does it, and they do it everywhere, always. Something about them is causing it.

Low consistency generally points to a one-off circumstance rather than a stable cause either way.

If a question gives you three pieces of information about a behavior across people, situations, and time, it is testing Kelley. Sort them into the three categories and read off the answer.

## Fundamental Attribution Error

**Overweighting dispositional causes when explaining other people's behavior, and underweighting situational ones.**

Someone cuts you off in traffic and you conclude they are a jerk, rather than that they might be rushing to a hospital. You see the person clearly and the situation poorly, so the person absorbs the explanation.

This is the most-tested item in the whole topic. Key restriction: it applies to *others'* behavior.

The standard explanation is perceptual salience. The actor is the figure; the situation is the ground. Whatever is in your attentional foreground gets the causal credit.

It is also culturally variable — the effect is stronger in individualist cultures than in collectivist ones, where situational explanations come more readily. This shows up in passages contrasting Western and East Asian samples.

## Actor-Observer Bias

The extension: **dispositional for others, situational for yourself.**

You attribute your own lateness to traffic and someone else's lateness to disorganization.

Two mechanisms are usually cited. First, informational — you know your own history and can see that you are usually punctual, while you have no such data on the other person. Second, perceptual — when you act, your visual field is filled with the situation, not with yourself.

The distinction from FAE: FAE is only about explaining others. Actor-observer is the comparison between how you explain others and how you explain yourself.

## Self-Serving Bias

**Internal attributions for your successes, external attributions for your failures.**

You aced the test because you're smart. You bombed it because the professor writes bad questions.

This is motivational rather than perceptual — it protects self-esteem. Note that it runs *opposite* to actor-observer for the success case: actor-observer predicts you explain your own behavior situationally, but self-serving bias predicts you take internal credit when things go well. The resolution is that self-serving bias is about valence. Good outcomes get pulled inward, bad outcomes pushed outward.

Depression is associated with a reversed pattern — internal, stable, global attributions for failure — which connects to learned helplessness.

## Optimism Bias and Just-World

**Optimism bias** — believing you are less likely than others to experience negative events. Smokers underestimating their own cancer risk.

**Just-world hypothesis** — the belief that people get what they deserve. This produces victim-blaming: if something bad happened to someone, they must have done something to warrant it. It is dispositional attribution applied defensively, because the alternative (bad things happen randomly) is threatening.

Just-world shows up in passages about attitudes toward poverty, illness, or crime victims.

## Related Concepts That Cluster With This

**Locus of control** (Rotter) — a stable personality trait rather than a situational attribution. Internal locus: outcomes result from your own actions. External locus: outcomes result from luck, fate, or powerful others. Internal locus correlates with better academic and health outcomes.

Do not confuse locus of control with attribution style. Attribution is about a specific event; locus of control is a general disposition.

**Attribute substitution** — when a question is hard, people unconsciously answer an easier one instead. This underlies several heuristics.

**Halo effect** — a global positive impression of someone bleeds into judgments of specific unrelated traits. Attractive people are rated as more intelligent and kinder. This is dispositional attribution generalized past the available evidence.

## The Discrimination Questions

Passages usually give you a scenario and four bias options. The fast route:

1. **Whose behavior?** Own → self-serving or actor-observer. Someone else's → FAE.
2. **Success or failure?** If the explanation shifts based on outcome valence → self-serving.
3. **Is it a comparison?** If both self and other are being explained differently → actor-observer.
4. **Is the target a victim?** → just-world.
5. **Three data points about consensus/distinctiveness/consistency?** → Kelley.

The most common trap is FAE vs. actor-observer. If the scenario only involves explaining someone else, it is FAE. If it explicitly contrasts self-explanation with other-explanation, it is actor-observer.

The second trap is self-serving vs. actor-observer for your own behavior. Check whether the outcome was good or bad. Self-serving is outcome-dependent; actor-observer is not.
